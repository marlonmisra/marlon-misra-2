Peter Thiel - Zero to one


Summary

Thiel writes about his world beliefs with a focus on startups and entrepreneurship. In the first chapters, he suggests that there are two types of growth - vertical growth (technology) and horizontal growth (globalization). He argues that vertical growth is more important because technology results in humanity getting more for less. In contrast, horizontal growth is merely copying. If the entire world only relied on horizontal progress, we would eventually reach a static state. I think it’s important to think about the two types of growth as distinct but I still think that horizontal growth is extremely important. If every country caught up to the USA then we would be in a much better place.

He goes on to explain that negative perceptions of monopolies are often unjustified. Many economics students learn that perfect competition is a preferred state because monopolies control prices, make large profits, and generally screw consumers. However, the statement is only true in an economy where nothing changes. In reality, creative monopolies create value where it didn’t exist before. In any case, from the entrepreneur's perspective you should always aim for monopoly. With monopoly profits, a company can afford to treat employees better, invest in more R&D, and generally be in a better position. In order to actually start a future monopoly business, you have to start in sufficiently small and growing market. Doing this ensures less competition from big players which is key. Facebook, for example, did this by initially focusing on a single university.

Thiel tries to categorize countries’ mindsets on a simple 2x2 matrix with definitiness and optimism scales. Today, China has definite pessimism because they have too many people and are merely copying other states. Europe has indefinite pessimism because they are not innovating and don’t know what to do about it. Thiel says this is why they barely work and love vacations. The US in the 1950 and 1960 had definite optimism because they just won World War 2 and invested heavily in the Internet, space technology, etc. The US today has indefinite optimism. We expect things to get better because our parents grew up during a time where it seemed to happen automatically. In order to have definite optimism again, we have to plan better and believe in a definite future. What we don’t need more of is bankers or consultants. Ivy-league graduates jump to these careers because they seek optionality. Once they start their jobs, they diversify again because they don’t know what the future entails. Well-roundedness, I believe, is vastly overrated and Thiel seems to agree by refering to it as “seeking optionality”.

Successful startups have 4 main traits: proprietary technology, economies of scale, network effects, and a strong brand. In addition, Thiel offers a set of questions every entrepreneur should answer: the technology question, monopoly question, team question, time question, durability question, and the distribution question. Successful startups should aim to be the last mover in a market because then the company can sustain profits.

Thiel also offers tactical suggestions for startup CEOs. Real technologists shouldn’t wear suits because they don’t need to fake the illusion of knowledge. Early on, CEOs should give each person unique responsibilities to make decision-making faster. They should take a low salary because it incentives long-term thinking and sets an example for others. They should be stealthy and avoid media because this only attracts competition. CEOs should create a mission-oriented cult-like atmosphere where everyone is extremely passionate about the goal. Public image should be taken seriously and one way to do that is to think about it early. The most important lesson for entrepreneurs is to reason from the ground up and think from first principles. World-changing discoveries are secrets that will only be found that way.

In the near future, Thiel beliefs that most value will come from technology companies that work at the intersection of computers and humans. His belief stems from the fact that humans and machines are good at opposite things (intentionality and creativity vs. data analysis). Thiel is optimistic about the near term because machines don’t compete with humans for resources or employment. In the long term, Thiel beliefs in one of two outcomes: The first is strong AI that kills us all and the second is utopia where machines act as the ultimate compliment of humans.

Lessons

  • Creative monopolies are good for everyone.

  • Start companies in small and growing markets.

  • Be stealthy and don’t disrupt.

  • Have definite optimism about the world.

  • Make people responsible for unique tasks.

  • Create a mission-oriented culture.

  • Computers don’t compete for resources.

  • Startups need proprietary tech, scale economies, network effects, and strong brands.
