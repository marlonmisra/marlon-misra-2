Seneca - Letters from a stoic


Summary

Seneca was a statesman, philosopher, and stoic who was born in Spain around 4 BC. The stoics believe that destructive emotions resulted from errors in judgement and that a person of moral and intellectual perfection doesn’t need to suffer. Seneca said that there is nothing good or bad and that it is only our thinking which makes it so. He believed that most people are tormented by the past and present because memories bring the agony of fear and foresight brings it prematurely. What one should do, he argued, is to confine unhappiness to the present. In practice, I think this is very difficult to do. Still, when bad things happen I remind myself that mental suffering is not a required step. Sometimes doing that helps. Other times, letting my emotions free works better.

The stoics believe that we should live in conformity with nature. If you live in harmony with nature you will never be poor but if you live in accord with people’s opinion, you will never be rich. For that reason I think it’s helpful to keep some things to yourself. If you share every word you write and every picture you take, you can expect opinions to overwhelm and control you.

Seneca didn’t want to be remembered by his money or power and firmly believed that happiness and wealth aren’t related. He thought that the issue with material things is that they can be taken away at any time. Nowadays, people say “invest in experiences and not things”. This idea has similar groundings because memories also cannot be taken away. Seneca believed that true happiness lies within and should only require necessities like food and water. His position was extreme but I think that many people would be equally happy with much less.

Seneca said to avoid craving for attention and other means of self-advertising. He suggested to avoid shabby attire, an outspoken dislike of something, and sleeping on the ground. More generally, he thought that inwardly everything should be different but our outward face should conform with the crowd. Our aim in life should not be diametrically opposed to, but better than, the crowd. I like this passage because I never realized that you can think of the “inward” and “outward” as completely distinct.

On friendship and trust, he said that one should live in a way where there is nothing which you can’t tell your enemy. Trusting everyone isn’t right but it is much better than trusting no one. Real friends should not be cultivated for the sake of usefulness. Doing that is a business deal and robs friendship of all its dignity. He said that people who travel all the time have plenty of acquaintances but no real friendships. While he emphasized the importance of relationships, he also believed that the ability to spend time alone is proof of a well ordered mind. I agree with both points and have tried to spend more time with fewer people in the last year. I’ve also shared more vulnerable things. So far I’m happy with the results.

Seneca speaks of mentorship and suggested that we should keep someone constantly before our eyes. Then we can live as if this person was watching us and use them as a standard against which we measure ourselves. Seneca also said that one should not form a hasty belief in oneself. It’s important to perform close scrutinies of oneself to see if one is really advancing in philosophy or only actual years. One way to do this that has worked for me is to constantly seek out criticism from people that you trust.

Seneca wrote about old age and how you should cherish it. Late in life many people stop desiring any pleasures. He wrote about how nice it is to have outworn one’s desires and leave them behind. Regarding death, many worry that old age brings death closer and closer. The truth is that death should be before the eyes of a young man as much as an old man. Because when you die, death will deliver the final verdict. All opinions, expectations, pride, and fear of failure will just wash away so you should do what truly matters before then. One should aim to have a lasting impact and to create enduring good.

Death follows but it also proceeds life. Is a lamp worse off when it is put out? We are the same way and asking the question doesn’t get us anywhere. And when one of our loved ones die, the pain is not without a pleasure of its own. In the memory of departed friends there is a resemblance to the way in which bitter fruits are agreeable. When you still have friends in your life, you’re worried that you’ll lose them. Once you lose them, you can keep the feeling of them with you. My middle school friend died years ago but everyone still remembers him. In many ways he lives on through us.

Seneca said that each day you should acquire something that gives you perspective. For example, you could experience poverty. He proposes that we set aside some time to live with the plainest of food and coarse clothing and ask ourselves whether this is what one used to dread. It is in times of security that one should prepare themselves for difficult times. I’ve tried some experiments that are somewhat related to this. For 3 months I slept on hardwood floor and another month I only took ice cold showers. These are small things but it’s amazing how much more you appreciate something when you get it back.

Seneca believes that good delivery is paramount in a philosopher. Even if one is carried away by passion, he should not increase his pace or speak faster than the capacity of the ear. You should be a slow-speaking person. Personally, this has been one of my biggest weaknesses. It takes conscious effort to change this but after a few months of consciously trying I have seem some improvement. In addition to a slow pace, I find conciseness to be equally important. There is no reason to use 20 words when you can say the same thing in 5.

Lessons

  • Destructive emotions come from false judgements.

  • Speak slowly.

  • Develop lasting friendships.

  • Cherish old age.

  • Acquire perspective each day.

  • Perform close scrutinies of yourself and welcome negative feedback.

  • Create enduring good to bring meaning to life.

  • Make your outward face conform with the crowd.

  • Spend time alone.

  • Everything will wash away when you die so do what you love before.

  • Live in accord with nature and not with peoples' opinions.

  • The Stoics saw the world as a single great community in which all men are brothers, ruled by a supreme providence which could be spoken of, almost according to choice or context, under a variety of names or descriptions including the divine reason, creative reason, nature, the spirit or purpose of the universe, destiny, a personal god, even (by way of concession to traditional religion) ‘the gods’. It is man’s duty to live in conformity with the divine will, and this means, firstly, bringing his life into line with ‘nature’s laws’, and secondly, resigning himself completely and uncomplainingly to whatever fate may send him. Only by living thus, and not setting too high a value on things which can at any moment be taken away from him, can he discover that true, unshakeable peace and contentment to which ambition, luxury and above all avarice are among the greatest obstacles.

  • Living ‘in accordance with nature’ means not only questioning convention and training ourselves to do without all except the necessities (plain food, water, basic clothing and shelter) but developing the inborn gift of reason which marks us off as different from the animal world. We are meant to set free or perfect this rational element, this particle of the universal reason, the ‘divine spark’ in our human make-up, so that it may campaign against and conquer pain, grief, superstition and the fear of death. It will show us that ‘there’s nothing either good or bad but thinking makes it so’, discipline the pleasures and the passions, and generally subordinate the body and emotions to the mind and soul.

  • This, the summum bonum or ‘supreme ideal’, is usually summarized in ancient philosophy as a combination of four qualities: wisdom (or moral insight), courage, self-control and justice (or upright dealing).   • I ask you, wouldn’t you say that anyone who took the view that a lamp was worse off when it was put out than it was before it was lit was an utter idiot? We, too, are lit and put out. We suffer somewhat in the intervening period, but at either end of it there is a deep tranquillity. For, unless I’m mistaken, we are wrong, my dear Lucilius, in holding that death follows after, when in fact it precedes as well as succeeds. Death is all that was before us. What does it matter, after all, whether you cease to be or never begin, when the result of either is that you do not exist?

  • I’ve just this moment returned from a ride in my sedanchair, feeling as tired as if I’d walked the whole distance instead of being seated all the way. Even to be carried for any length of time is hard work, and all the more so, I dare say, because it is unnatural, nature having given us legs with which to do our own walking, just as she gave us eyes with which to do our own seeing. Soft living imposes on us the penalty of debility; we cease to be able to do the things we’ve long been grudging about doing.

  • The only true serenity is the one which represents the free development of a sound mind. Look at the man whose quest for sleep demands absolute quiet from his spacious house. To prevent any sound disturbing his ears every one of his host of slaves preserves total silence and those who come anywhere near him walk on tip-toe. Naturally enough he tosses from side to side, trying to snatch some fitful sleep in between the spells of fretting, and complains of having heard sounds when he never heard them at all. And what do you suppose is the reason? His mind is in a ferment. It is this which needs to be set at peace. Here is the mutiny that needs to be suppressed. The fact that the body is lying down is no reason for supposing that the mind is at peace. Rest is sometimes far from restful. Hence our need to be stimulated into general activity and kept occupied and busy with pursuits of the right nature whenever we are victims of the sort of idleness that wearies of itself.
