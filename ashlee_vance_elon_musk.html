Ashlee Vance - Elon Musk


Summary

Elon’s companies are changing the world. Below I explain the reasons that I think led to his success as an entrepreneur. With SpaceX, Tesla, Solarcity, Paypal, and Zip2, there are certainly lessons to take away.

In interviews and in this book, Elon often refers to a first-principles approach to problem solving. Equivalent phrases he uses are “boil it down to the physics”, “figure out the most fundamental truths and reason up”, or “ask the right questions first”. While most people are quick to deem things impossible, they often aren’t sufficiently rigorous in their reasoning. For example, it’s easy to say that rockets are expensive to build because they have always been expensive. It’s harder to determine the material constituents of a rocket, determine their prices, and then meticulously research and calculate the lowest-cost process to put them together. Contrary to the lean startup methodology that’s currently trendy, I think this process is better for complex problems. People that start successful companies work on them for decades. Considering that, taking the time to do this kind of analysis is worth it.

The trait that consistently comes up in this book is Elon’s courage. He credits his rough upbringing to this skill. He was bullied, had a brutal father, and life at home and at school was terrible. Later he had to deal with 3 divorces, losing a child, getting crushed by the press, and almost losing Tesla and SpaceX in the recession. With these experiences, his pain tolerance got better and better. Elon’s friends say that in stressful situations he becomes forward-looking and hyperrational. He just does what it takes whether that means working 23-hour days, investing everything to keep 2 companies alive simultaneously, or convincing the girl he likes to go out with him. Once he proves something is possible, he just demonstrates a crazy level of conviction. Like many of his employees say, “Never tell him something is impossible or come without a backup plan”.

Elon’s raison d’être is to make humans an interplanetary species and power the entire planet with solar power. This worldview is so crazy and noble that no company in my mind has anywhere close to those ambitions. People often wonder how Elon can be so demanding and hard on employees and get away with it. I think the reason is his companies’ missions. Imagine Coca Cola’s CEO asking a regular employee to start working 16 hours a day and 7 days a week. You can only do that with an important selfless mission. Elon even says that there are too many people who work on Internet stuff, finance, and law. A mission-oriented culture makes everything else easier.

Elon is pragmatic. As a young student, he only worked hard in courses that he thought were important. He barely passed other ones and read books instead. In university, he called CEOs and other important people and asked them if they would meet with him to chat. These people usually agreed because they never got these types of requests. As he got older, he nurtured his reputation because he knew it would pay off later. He hired documentary crews to tell his story and always asked his PR teams to “fix negative news stories”. Elon is soft-spoken in interviews and is likely very diplomatic when dealing with external stakeholders. Getting a $465M loan from the government (Tesla) and partnering with NASA (SpaceX) are exceedingly difficult. Elon knows how to play the game.

From a young age, Elon obsessed about learning and self-improvement. On many days he locked himself in a room and read for 10 hours. When he ran SpaceX he started with little knowledge about the business. But with time, he read every rocketry book and brought in his best engineers so that they could teach him technical details. After a few years he became an aerospace expert. In interviews he always says that the best way to learn is to “read a lot and talk to smart people”.

Elon’s companies value vertical integration and in-house manufacturing. At SpaceX, 90% of rockets are manufactured in-house whereas their main competitor ULA works with 1,200 suppliers. At Tesla, retail stores and free Superchargers go against convention completely. Doing these things has resulted in better products, lower costs, and fewer dependencies. If a company has better people, it should do as much as possible alone if it can afford to. In addition to vertical integration and in-house manufacturing, Elon also has a very hands-on management style. He’s deeply involved in engineering and design, and once described himself as a “nanomanager” in an interview. Most people would say that micromanagement is bad. I think the effectiveness of Elon’s style is dependent on the complexity of the task. For something as complex as a rocket, it’s probably better. In another interview I watched, Peter Thiel called Tesla’s and SpaceX’s innovation style as “complex coordination”. He says Elon excels at taking disparate but already functional pieces and puts them together in a beautiful way. To do that effectively, I think that being a hands-on controlling leader is valuable.

Lessons

  • Reason from first principles.

  • Be courageous, work hard, and have conviction.

  • Important missions can make people work harder.

  • Be pragmatic and focus on things that matter.

  • Be a lifelong learner by reading like crazy and talking to smart people.

  • Vertical integration and in-house work can be major competitive advantages.

  • Micromanagement can be good for complex tasks.
