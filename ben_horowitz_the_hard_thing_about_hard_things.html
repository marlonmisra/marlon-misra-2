Ben Horowitz - The hard thing about hard things


Summary

Ben Horowitz is a cofounder of venture capital firm Andreessen Horowitz. In The Hard Thing About Hard Things, he shares his experience from Netscape, Loudcloud, and Opsware. The main reason I like this book is because it’s aimed at startups. Most similar books are written by consultants and are only relevant for large peacetime companies.

The best CEOs exhibit brilliance and courage. Brilliance is straightforward but courage is underestimated. Running companies is extremely difficult and requires high pain tolerance. Ben was in countless situations where his company would have died in weeks where it not for a highly controversial decision that he made. CEOs should make important final decisions alone because they are the only ones who have full knowledge of the business. Therefore, when the board and investors disagree with you, and you are only slightly in favor of option A, you should still generally proceed. This is the best way to become courageous.

Courage also relates to the mindset of there being a definite future. It’s important to have faith in the future. Even preparing for a negative plan B can psychologically put you in a bad position. From Ben’s experience, the most common thing successful CEOs claim is that they never gave up. Founding CEOs tend be better than professional CEOs precisely because of their courage.

For a company to be successful, it’s important to have a mission that’s so important that it supersedes everyone’s personal ambition. This will make everything else (recruiting, retention, culture, etc.) easier. For the product, it has to be significantly better than the current way of doing that thing. If that’s not the case, other big companies will crush you. For that reason, it’s important to be focused and get one thing right. Being super dominant in one thing is generally much better than being slightly above average in ten.

Ben gives many management tips. One piece of advice I really liked is to try and look at situations through as many perspectives a possible. This helps to separate fact and fiction. For example, if someone wants a promotion, think about how that would affect his coworkers. Another tip is to share negative news openly when speaking to employees. Being overly positive is a cowardly thing to do and openness shows confidence. In board meetings, insert an agenda item titled “What are we not doing” to help long-term thinking.

Ben gives many tips for hiring. He suggests that you should always look for extreme strengths rather than a lack of weakness. This is better because it is not good enough to build an average company. You want experts in each of your functions. Companies often win because they have one silver bullet like explained above. For executive hiring, consensus decisions always sway the process away from strength and toward lack of weakness. That is why the ultimate hiring decision should be made solo. You also want to be careful about management debt. Hiring is one of the areas where it’s extremely important not to rush. When new employees join, the Zuck approach is relevel them and not give out glamorous roles. This is the fair thing to do.

Training is one of the highest leverage activities that a manager can perform. Managers should have one-on-one meeting with their direct reports and the meetings should be owned by the report. CEOs should have opinions on everything and constantly be evaluating direct reports. Using the shit sandwich is good for junior employees but may not work for executives.

For advancement, you want to be strict with your processes to avoid building a political organization. When people ask for raises or promotions you should not agree to them because it sets a terrible precedent. When deciding whether to promote someone from within or on the outside, think about how important inside knowledge is for the role. Senior people should be hired because of time. You’re always racing against time.

For firing, Ben provides the following advice. First, managers should do their own firings. In the final meeting, managers should explain that this is company failure, that the decision is nonnegotiable, and be prepared with details about the benefits. For executive firing, managers should frame the layoff as an integration problem. Two common reasons why people quit are bad managers and lack of learning. One way to tackle both is great training.

Scaling is difficult and people will have to be replaced in the process. The big issues are communication, common knowledge, and decision-making. You have to decide whether to organize the company around functions (sales, product management, etc.) or self-contained business units. To answer the question, focus on the desired output first.

There is a big difference between peacetime CEOs and wartime CEOs. Both require different management techniques. For example, when Jobs returned to Apple and there were weeks of cash left, everyone had to focus and listen to Steve without questioning. In contrast, when Google instigated 20% time, that was a classic example of a company trying to figure out what to do next in a slow creative way.

Regarding exits, a company should stay independent if it is very early on in a soon-to-be very large market and has a good chance of being number one. If a potential acquirer approaches, a good answer is "We are not for sale." It conveys that you aren’t ready to sell but it but it also leaves the door open to a particularly aggressive buyer.

Lessons

  • Brilliance and courage are the key CEO traits.

  • Great missions supersede personal interests.

  • Startups need to be 10X better than the status quo.

  • Look at difficult situations from many perspectives.

  • Hire for extreme strength and not a lack of weakness.

  • Training is one of the highest leverage activities for managers.

  • Have strict processes (hiring, promotions, etc.) to avoid politics.

  • Peacetime and wartime leadership is completely different.

  • Stay independent if you are early in a large market and will likely be #1.

  • One challenge is the Peter Principle. Coined by Dr. Laurence J.   • Peacetime in business means those times when a company has a large advantage over the competition in its core market, and its market is growing. In times of peace, the company can focus on expanding the market and reinforcing the company's strengths. In wartime, a company is fending off an imminent existential threat. Such a threat can come from a wide range of sources, including competition, dramatic macroeconomic change, market change, supply chain change, and so forth.

    • In peacetime, leaders must maximize and broaden the current opportunity. As a result, peacetime leaders employ techniques to encourage broad-based creativity and contribution across a diverse set of possible objectives. In wartime, by contrast, the company typically has a single bullet in the chamber and must, at all costs, hit the target. The company's survival in wartime depends upon strict adherence and alignment to the mission. When Steve Jobs returned to Apple, the company was weeks away from bankruptcy--a classic wartime scenario. He needed everyone to move with precision and follow his exact plan; there was no room for individual creativity outside the core mission. In stark contrast, as Google achieved dominance in the search market, Google's management fostered peacetime innovation by enabling and even requiring every employee to spend 20 percent of their time on their own new projects.
