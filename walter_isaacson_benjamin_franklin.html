Walter Isaacson - Benjamin Franklin


Summary

During Benjamin Franklin’s life, he was America’s best diplomat, scientist, writer, businessman, and political thinker. It’s hard to think of anyone alive today that has such diverse talents. I think the main reason is that it now takes many years of specialization to push boundaries in any field. People nowadays are also more conservative. It's easy to view problems as solved or unsolvable and then not make the effort to try.

Franklin’s two core principles were industry and frugality because in his words they are means of procuring wealth and securing virtue. Securing virtue through frugality makes sense to me. Being frugal lets you avoid entering a cycle of chasing things you don’t want.

One of his most admirable traits was his obsession with self-improvement. Franklin would read essays, take notes, set them aside, and try to recreate the essay in his own words. I’m doing the same thing as I write this and the benefits are clear. When you only read a book you don’t engage deeply with the material and forget most of it. When you follow Franklin’s steps you store more knowledge in memory, relate better to other concepts, and think of new ideas more effectively. Franklin also made lists of virtues and deliberately set time aside to work on each one. Again, I’ve noticed that doing this is effective because it takes deliberate effort over long time periods to adopt new habits or lose bad ones.

In addition to self-improvement, Franklin nurtured his reputation for pride and utility purposes. He realized that people are more likely to admire your work if you can keep them from feeling jealous. He noted that you can become more revered by displaying self-deprecating humor, unpretentious demeanor, and unaggressive style in conversation. I’ve noticed these things work really well too. The simplest way to do this is to take blame for bad things and give credit for good things. It also helps to be a really good listener. Everyone likes people that listen.

Another recurring theme in Franklin’s life was pragmatism. Franklin always worked on the most impactful things of the time, regardless of how different they were. He poked fun at professions of faith because they didn’t serve any worldly purpose. Franklin also wanted to be useful after death. He accomplished this by designating $2,000 as loans for apprenticeships.

Franklin’s diplomatic and Socratic techniques proved successful with America’s alliance with France and treaty with England. When he was young, he did mock debates and learned that being less contentious and confrontational had many benefits. In arguments, he would ask innocent questions and draw people into making concessions, which would gradually prove the point he was trying to make. During conferences, his tone of moderation and absence of one-sided advocacy led to creative compromise. Asking questions to get someone to agree with you is so counterintuitive that it’s hard to practice. I think this is an effective method when you don’t have time constraints.

Franklin’s economic conservatism was balanced by his moral belief that actions should be judged by how much they benefit the common good. He thought that people who acquired more wealth than needed had a duty to help those in genuine need and to create civic institutions. But those who couldn’t pull their weight from a lack of virtue should not receive help from society. I think I’m in favor of moderate welfare expenditures. You want to help those who really need it and want to limit exploiters as much as possible. Otherwise things stop being fair for everyone. It would be interesting to look at studies of pre-welfare and post-welfare countries to see how effective government programs are.

Franklin was dubious of populism and inheritance. These beliefs were interwoven in the 1776 Declaration Of Independence and the 1777 Articles Of Confederation. I really don’t like inheritances. So many people get a massive head start without doing any work. Because of that, they often exhibit entitlement. All things considered, I don’t know how you would fix inheritance in a way that most people would agree. Life is unfair.

Franklin was a strong believer in social mobility and resistance to authority. He believed that rights and power should be based on merit and hard work and not the results of heritage. The trait ‘resistance to authority’ seems to be present in most successful people. Steve Jobs, Elon Musk, etc. I keep seeing it in biographies.

The most interesting historical note in the biography is related to the Senate and House of Representatives. Because small states and large states kept arguing over money and liberties, a dual system was agreed upon. The House gives power to large states who are concerned with financials. The Senate gives power to small states who are worried about liberties. In order to pass any bill, the House and Senate have to accept the proposal. The two-system decision has proven incredibly successful and Franklin played an important part in implementing it.

Lessons

  • Frugality affords liberty and peace of mind.

  • Resist to arbitrary authority.

  • Use the Socratic method in debates.

  • Take blame and give credit.

  • Improve yourself by reading, writing, and working on virtues.

  • Actions should be judged by how much they benefit the common good.

Similarly, he helped to create, and came to symbolize, a new political order in which rights and power were based not on the happen-stance of heritage but on merit and virtue and hard work. He rose up the social ladder, from runaway apprentice to dining with kings, in a way that would become quintessentially American. But in doing so he resolutely resisted, as a matter of principle, sometimes to a fur-capped extreme, taking on elitist pretensions.

  • Through it all, he trusted the hearts and minds of his fellow leather-aprons more than he did those of any inbred elite. He saw middle-class values as a source of social strength, not as something to be derided. His guiding principle was a "dislike of everything that tended to debase the spirit of the common people.
